No one enjoys penalties. They slow the game down, give one team an advantage, and are almost always controversial. They also protect players and preserve the integrity of the sport, which is precisely why they remain essential. The National Football League has faced countless complaints about officiating in recent years, but given the necessity of officials, it may be time to shift the focus away from the referees and onto the calls themselves.

So much can be done by looking at common penalty types, teams, and locations on the field. By identifying trends in these variables, teams can find problem spots and work on cleaning up their play. This could give them a leg up on their competition and even change the outcome of some close games.

A graph of all penalty locations throughout the 2025 season (so far) tells a very interesting story. It’s important to note that the “Yardline” variable means how far the offensive team is away from the opponent’s end zone. Therefore, a penalty at “Yardline = 99” would have happened while the offense was backed up at their own 1-yard line. “Yardline = 1” means the offense is at the opponent’s 1-yard line and is in prime scoring position. The “Yardline” variable is also marked at where the line of scrimmage is when the penalty occurs, not where exactly the penalty occurs. That’s why there are no end zones even though pass interference penalties regularly occur in the end zone.

The NFL also doesn’t track where the penalty occurs across the width of the field, so the vertical location on the field doesn’t matter. The points are just spread out to make them easier to distinguish from each other.

The first thing that stands out is the number of penalties at the 35-yard line. It may appear out of place at first, but looking deeper, it makes sense that so many penalties occur there. All kickoff-related penalties will occur at the kicking team’s 35, so they are all concentrated on that line. There are also fewer penalties the closer you get to “Yardline = 100.” This too makes sense as fewer plays are going to be run in that area of the field. Most plays will be run, and subsequently most penalties will occur, closer to midfield or the opponent side of the field.

After a deep dive into the location of penalties, it may be beneficial to look into team totals in both number of penalties and penalty yards. That gives a better individual picture of how a team is faring so far.

Code 
library(nflplotR)

teampenalties <- penalties %>%
  group_by(penalty_team) %>%
  summarise(Penalties=n(), Yards=sum(penalty_yards))
  
fit <- lm(Yards ~ Penalties, data = teampenalties)

#summary(fit)

penaltyplot <- ggplot(data=teampenalties, aes(x=Penalties, y=Yards)) + 
  geom_nfl_logos(aes(team_abbr = penalty_team), width = 0.05) + 
  theme_minimal() + 
  geom_smooth(data = teampenalties, aes(x = Penalties, y = Yards), method = "lm", color = "red") +
  labs(title="Team penalties appear consistent across the league", subtitle = "Jaguars struggle while Rams excel", caption="Source: nflfastR: Functions to Efficiently Access NFL Play by Play Data | By Dominic Sander") +
  theme(
    plot.title = element_text(size = 16, face = "bold"),
    axis.title = element_text(size = 10),
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size = 10),
    panel.grid.minor = element_blank()
  )

In general, it appears that team penalties follow a fairly linear trend (as modeled by the red line on the graph). As the number of penalties increases, naturally so does the number of penalty yards. The data shows that on average, for every penalty, the number of yards increases by 9.26.

No team really strays too far from what’s expected, but a few stick out as slightly above or below average. The Broncos are the furthest above the line, meaning for the number of penalties they committed, they were assessed more yards than would be expected. Teams like this generally commit more serious or costly penalties, like defensive pass interference or personal foul penalties.

Teams like the Saints and Cardinals were on the opposite end of the spectrum, committing less costly penalties like “False Starts” and “Offsides.”

The Jaguars also seem to separate themselves above the rest in terms of number of penalties and total penalty yards—a place no team wants to be. The Rams, on the other hand, clearly have the fewest penalties and penalty yards.

The graphic above is an RShiny app created to show how all 32 NFL teams have fared so far in 2025. The ten most common penalty types, as well as an aggregate of all other penalties are the options one can select from. There’s a lot to explore, but some of the biggest takeaways are that the Broncos lead the league in “Defensive Pass Interference” penalties, Chicago leads in “Roughing the Passer” penalties, and the Rams consistently make the fewest mistakes across almost all penalty types.

In general, a lot can be said about how penalties have affected the NFL so far this year. A kickoff rule update created a dramatic shift in where penalties are occurring and even introduced the new penalty, “Kickoff Short of Landing Zone.” Teams also follow a fairly linear trend in yards per penalty, showing that no one team stands out in particular for committing more of the egregious penalties, such as “Personal Fouls” or “Defensive Pass Interferences.’ It also means that no team was often committing less costly penalties, like”Offsides” or “False starts.” A team-by-team breakdown can also determine where each franchise needs to improve penalty-wise.

While the season isn’t over, a deep dive into penalties across the league could help a team in their final push for a first-round bye, divisional title, or even playoff spot.
